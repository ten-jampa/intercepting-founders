# Intercepting Founders: Where F-Prime Could Have Met the AI 50

**Source**: Brian Halligan (@bhalligan) tweet — "21 of the Forbes 50 AI companies had founders that went to school in Boston/Cambridge. #PrimordialSoup"

**Compiled for**: F-Prime Capital (Boston-based VC)
**Date**: 2026-03-25

---

## Executive Summary

Of the 21 Forbes AI 50 companies with Boston/Cambridge school connections, we identified:

- **10 companies** with MIT-connected founders (Cursor, Scale AI, Kalshi, Codeium, Cerebras, Coactive AI, Databricks, OpenAI, Together AI, and Suno via MIT Sloan)
- **11 companies** with Harvard-connected founders (Etched, OpenEvidence, Pika, Decagon, LangChain, Writer, Speak, Suno, Perplexity AI, OpenAI, and Suno)
- **1 company** with Boston University connection (Captions)
- **4 companies** whose founders started their companies while still in school (marked with asterisk *)
- **2 companies** where the Boston/Cambridge connection could not be verified from public sources (Crusoe, Snorkel AI)

The single most actionable finding: **Kensho Technologies in Cambridge, MA** was a hidden talent nexus — Daniel Nadler founded it while a Harvard PhD student, and all 4 Suno founders worked there before founding their $500M+ AI music company. F-Prime's geographic advantage in Boston is structurally underexploited.

## Part 1: Company Deep Dives

### 1. Anysphere (Cursor) * — MIT

**The Story**: Four MIT Class of 2022 classmates — all computer science and math students — incorporated Anysphere while still undergrads. Their first idea was AI for mechanical engineering (CAD autocomplete). They pivoted to AI-assisted coding and built Cursor, which became the fastest-growing B2B SaaS product in history, hitting $500M ARR in 21 months.

**Founders & MIT Details**:
| Founder | MIT Degree | Pre-MIT Signal |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| Michael Truell (CEO) | BS CS '22 | USA Computing Olympiad finalist; interned at Google, Two Sigma |
| Sualeh Asif (CPO) | BS CS & Math '22 | International Mathematical Olympiad (Pakistan); interned at IBM |
| Arvid Lunnemark | BS Math & CS '22 (double) | International Olympiad in Informatics medalist; interned at Stripe, Jane Street |
| Aman Sanger | BS CS '22 | Worked at Google, Bridgewater; ran AI consultancy |

**Programs**: Neo Scholars (Ali Partovi's mentorship network for technical undergrads)
**Accelerator**: OpenAI Startup Fund/Accelerator (2023, $8M seed)
**Labs**: No specific MIT lab confirmed; collaborated on "research and projects at MIT"

**Interception Window**: 2018-2022 (MIT undergrad years). The ideal moment was early 2022 — company incorporated, $400K pre-seed raised, founders still on campus. A VC embedded in the MIT CS community, Neo Scholars network, or MIT hackathon circuit could have been the first call.

**F-Prime Angle**: These founders were visible through Olympiad pipelines and the Neo Scholars program. A systematic relationship with Neo and MIT EECS would have surfaced them.

### 9. Etched * — Harvard

**The Story**: Three Harvard undergrads — Gavin Uberti ('25), Chris Zhu ('24), and Robert Wachen ('24) — incorporated Etched in October 2022 as a dorm room project. In March 2023, Uberti and Zhu were still taking math and CS classes. In April 2023, they closed a $5.36M seed round and dropped out. All three became 2024 Thiel Fellows.

**Interception Window**: October 2022 to April 2023 — a 6-month window while founders were still Harvard students. A VC with campus presence could have encountered them. Their paper showing 140x performance/dollar improvement over GPUs was the founding insight.

**F-Prime Angle**: Harvard undergrad dorm-room projects in AI hardware. Monitor SEAS math/CS students, especially those writing technical papers outside of class. The Thiel Fellowship announcement was a public signal but came after the seed round.

## Part 2: Cluster Analysis

### Cluster 1: MIT EECS — The Primary Pipeline
**Companies**: Anysphere (Cursor), Scale AI, Kalshi, Codeium (Windsurf), Coactive AI, Cerebras Systems
**Founders interceptable**: ~12
**Peak years**: 2014-2022

This is the single most productive pipeline. MIT EECS produces an outsized share of AI founders. The pattern is clear: technically exceptional students (often Olympiad medalists) who start building during or immediately after their undergrad years.

**Key sub-signals within MIT EECS**:
- SuperUROP participants (Kalshi's Tarek Mansour)
- Neo Scholars (Cursor's entire founding team)
- QuestBridge Scholars (Coactive's Cody Coleman)
- International Olympiad pipelines (Cursor's Lunnemark, Asif)
- Students taking 5+ grad courses as undergrads (Scale's Alexandr Wang)

### Cluster 2: Harvard CS / SEAS — The Researcher Pipeline
**Companies**: Etched, Pika, Decagon, LangChain, Perplexity AI
**Founders interceptable**: ~7
**Peak years**: 2015-2023

Harvard produces a different type of founder than MIT — more research-oriented, often with published papers and lab affiliations. The Harvard NLP Group (Alexander Rush) was a specific incubator for Pika's Demi Guo.

**Key sub-signals within Harvard CS**:
- Published researchers with high citation counts (Pika's Demi Guo: 5,985 citations)
- IOI/IMO gold medalists attending Harvard (Perplexity's Johnny Ho: 3x IOI gold)
- Students building side projects/startups during school (Etched, Decagon's Jesse Zhang with Lowkey)

### Cluster 3: Harvard Physics → AI — The Cross-Disciplinary Pipeline
**Companies**: Suno
**Founders interceptable**: 3
**Peak years**: 2010-2017

The physics-to-ML pipeline is underappreciated. Harvard's elite physics labs (Yacoby Lab, Lukin Group) produced founders who applied their quantitative rigor and systems thinking to AI. The MIT-Harvard Center for Ultracold Atoms (CUA) is a joint program worth monitoring.

### Cluster 4: Kensho Technologies — The Hidden Talent Nexus
**Companies**: Suno (all 4 founders), OpenEvidence (founder), LangChain (possible)
**Founders interceptable**: 5+
**Peak years**: 2013-2022

**This is the most actionable discovery in the entire analysis.** Kensho Technologies, a Cambridge-based AI company founded by Harvard PhD student Daniel Nadler in 2013, was a talent concentration point. When S&P Global acquired Kensho for $550M in 2018, it retained the team temporarily — but eventually, top talent departed to found new companies. The Suno founding team (all 4 members) came directly from Kensho.

**Implication for F-Prime**: Identify the "next Kensho" — Cambridge-based AI companies that concentrate elite technical talent. When these companies get acquired or talent starts departing, the founders of the next wave of companies are being formed.

### Cluster 5: Y Combinator Feeder (MIT/Harvard → YC)
**Companies**: Scale AI (YC S16), Kalshi (YC W19), Decagon's prior startup Lowkey (YC)
**Founders interceptable**: 5

MIT and Harvard students applying to Y Combinator is a compounding signal. The application to YC is often the last moment a Boston-based VC can intercept before the founder relocates to Silicon Valley.

### Cluster 6: Thiel Fellowship Signal
**Companies**: Etched (2024 class), Speak (2012 class)
**Founders interceptable**: 4

Harvard students receiving Thiel Fellowships are extreme outliers. The Fellowship announcement is a public signal, but the best VCs are already tracking the applicant pool.

## Part 3: F-Prime Interception Strategy

### Tier 1: Critical — Highest-ROI Activities

**1. Embed in MIT EECS (6+ companies, 12+ founders)**
- Attend MIT delta v Demo Day annually (September/October)
- Sponsor or attend MIT Hack events and EECS seminars
- Build relationships with MIT AI faculty (CSAIL professors)
- Monitor SuperUROP project presentations (spring semester)
- Track Neo Scholars cohorts at MIT each year
- Hold regular office hours at MIT Martin Trust Center for Entrepreneurship

**2. Embed in Harvard CS / SEAS (5+ companies, 7+ founders)**
- Guest lecture in Harvard CS courses (CS50, upper-level AI)
- Build relationship with AI-focused Harvard CS faculty
- Sponsor Harvard i-lab events and demo days
- Attend Harvard CS research showcases (especially NLP and systems)
- Monitor The Harvard Crimson for student startup coverage

**3. Map and Track Kensho Alumni Network (3+ companies, 5+ founders)**
- Maintain a relationship map of former Kensho/S&P Global AI employees in Cambridge
- Identify the "next Kensho" — Cambridge AI companies concentrating elite talent
- Monitor departures from Cambridge-based AI companies (potential future founders)
- Build relationships with current Cambridge AI startup founders

### Tier 2: High — Strong Supporting Activities

**4. Track the Olympiad-to-Boston Pipeline**
- IOI, IMO, and USACO medalists who enroll at MIT or Harvard
- These individuals disproportionately become AI founders
- Build relationships with the competitive programming community at both schools

**5. Monitor Harvard Physics → AI Career Pivots**
- Track Harvard Physics PhD graduates transitioning to ML/AI industry roles
- Build relationships with Yacoby Lab, Lukin Group, and CUA alumni
- The physics-to-ML career pivot is a high-signal pattern

**6. Pre-YC Interception**
- MIT/Harvard students applying to Y Combinator represent a last-chance window
- Build relationships with YC partners who see Boston-originating applications
- Offer local support that YC cannot (proximity, Boston-specific network)

### Tier 3: Medium — Worth Maintaining

**7. Harvard Innovation Lab (i-lab)**
- Student entrepreneurs pass through here; Connor Zwick (Speak) specifically credited it
- Good venue for meeting early-stage Harvard founders

**8. MIT Sloan / MIT Entrepreneurship Programs**
- Mikey Shulman (Suno) is an MIT Sloan lecturer
- MIT Sloan students with technical backgrounds are founder candidates

**9. Boston University CS**
- Lower yield but Gaurav Misra (Captions) came through BU
- Worth periodic engagement

### Timing Signals: When to Engage

| Signal | What It Means | Action |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| Student publishes paper with high early citations | Research talent, possible founder | Reach out for coffee, offer to discuss commercialization |
| Student enters Neo Scholars / QuestBridge | Already identified as exceptional | Build relationship early |

## Part 4: Key Patterns and Takeaways

### Pattern 1: The "Started in School" Premium
The asterisked companies (Cursor, Etched, OpenEvidence, Scale AI) were literally founded while founders were students. These represent the highest interception opportunity — the founders were physically in Cambridge, had not yet raised significant capital, and were most receptive to local VC relationships.

### Pattern 2: The 2-Year Post-Graduation Window
For companies not started in school (Codeium, Kalshi, Coactive), the typical gap between graduation and founding was 2-3 years. Tracking MIT/Harvard grads in their first 2-3 post-graduation years — especially those at Cambridge-based companies — is a productive strategy.

### Pattern 3: Talent Concentration Points (The Kensho Model)
Kensho Technologies concentrated Harvard/MIT talent in Cambridge, and when that talent dispersed, it created Suno and potentially others. F-Prime should systematically identify Cambridge AI companies that are concentrating exceptional talent, and build relationships with that talent before they leave to found their own companies.

### Pattern 4: The Cross-School Network
Several companies have founders from BOTH MIT and Harvard (OpenAI, Suno via MIT Sloan). The MIT-Harvard overlap (shared research centers like CUA, cross-registration, Cambridge social networks) creates a meta-network worth monitoring.

### Pattern 5: F-Prime's Structural Advantage
F-Prime is headquartered in Boston. Of the 21 companies, at least 19 had founders who were physically in Cambridge at some point. Most Silicon Valley VCs have no systematic presence on MIT or Harvard campuses. F-Prime's geographic proximity is a moat that is currently underexploited.
